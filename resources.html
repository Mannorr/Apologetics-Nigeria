Answering Islam · The Bible

Has the Bible been changed?

Swipe →

Muslims say the Bible was corrupted. Rewritten by human hands. No longer what God gave.

And that the Qur’an, unlike your book, was kept perfect.

It’s a fair challenge. It also falls apart fast.

Start with the Qur’an itself. It tells Christians to judge by the Gospel.

“And let the People of the Gospel judge by what Allah has revealed therein.”

That is Muhammad’s own book. Speaking to Christians in his own lifetime. About the Gospel they were holding.

Qur’an 5:47

It says the same to Jews and Christians together.

“You are standing on nothing until you uphold the Torah, the Gospel, and what has been revealed to you from your Lord.”

Uphold them. Obey them. This is the Qur’an calling the Torah and Gospel God’s word. Not lost. Not corrupt.

Qur’an 5:68

Here is the problem that creates.

If the Gospel was already corrupted, why send anyone to it? You don’t tell people to obey a book you say is broken.

So either the Gospel was trustworthy in the 600s, or the Qur’an pointed people to a lie. It cannot be both.

The Qur’an even tells the doubter where to check.

“So if you are in doubt about that which We have revealed to you, then ask those who have been reading the Scripture before you.”

Ask the people reading the earlier Scripture. The very book now said to be corrupt.

Qur’an 10:94

Now the evidence they rarely mention.

In 1947, a shepherd found jars of scrolls in a cave above the Dead Sea. Inside was a complete copy of Isaiah.

Written around 125 BC. Seven hundred years before Islam. Before Jesus was even born.

Scholars set it beside the Hebrew Bible we read today. A thousand years of copying stood between them.

About 95 percent identical. The differences were mostly spelling and the odd slip of the pen.

Nothing that touched the meaning. The text had held.

The New Testament is the best preserved book of the ancient world. It is not close.

More than 5,800 Greek copies survive. Some written within a couple of centuries of the events. Older than Islam itself.

No other ancient book has a fraction of that.

Are there differences between the copies? Yes. Spelling. Word order. A tired scribe’s mistake.

We can see every one of them, precisely because we have so many copies to compare.

Not a single Christian teaching rests on a disputed line.

So when was it corrupted?

Not after Muhammad. The copies already existed, centuries before him, saying what ours say.

To prove the Bible was changed, you would have to produce the real one it replaced. In fourteen hundred years, nobody has. It was never written.

And picture the scale.

By Muhammad’s day the Gospel was everywhere. In Greek, Latin, Syriac, Coptic. Across Africa, Asia and Europe. Thousands of copies, in different lands, in different languages.

You cannot round them all up and edit them into agreement. It is not possible.

So it wasn’t changed.

Which leaves the question that was always the real one.

Not whether the Bible is reliable. It is. Whether you will read what it says.

“The grass withers, the flower fades, but the word of our God stands forever.”

Isaiah 40:8

The Historical Jesus

Was Jesus real, or a myth?

Swipe →

Some say Jesus never walked the earth. That his followers invented him. A legend dressed up as a man.

It has a name. Mythicism. It is loud online.

It is also believed by no serious historian alive. Here is why.

Start with the most famous skeptic in the field. Bart Ehrman. An agnostic. The scholar your atheist friends quote.

He wrote an entire book to shut this claim down.

“He certainly existed, as virtually every competent scholar of antiquity, Christian or non-Christian, agrees.”

Bart Ehrman

That is not a lone opinion. It is the whole field.

There is more evidence for Jesus of Nazareth than for almost any other figure of the ancient world.

The handful who deny it are not doing history. They are doing ideology.

So set the New Testament aside for a moment.

Just read what his enemies wrote. Romans. Jews. Greeks. Syrians. None of them followers. Several of them hostile.

Every one of them treats him as a real man.

Tacitus was Rome’s greatest historian. He had no love for Christians. He called their faith a “mischievous superstition.” Around 116 AD, he still wrote this.

“Christus, from whom the name had its origin, suffered the extreme penalty during the reign of Tiberius at the hands of one of our procurators, Pontius Pilate.”

He names the man. The emperor. The governor. The place. A hostile witness with nothing to gain.

Tacitus, Annals 15.44

Josephus was a Jewish historian writing for Rome around 93 AD. Not a follower of Jesus. He mentions him twice.

“There was a wise man called Jesus. His conduct was good, and he was known to be virtuous. Many people became his disciples. Pilate condemned him to be crucified.”

Josephus, Antiquities 18.3.3

He mentions him again, in a line no scholar disputes.

“the brother of Jesus, who was called Christ, whose name was James.”

You do not record the brother of a man who never existed.

Josephus, Antiquities 20.9.1

Pliny was a Roman governor. Around 112 AD he wrote to the emperor, unsure what to do about the Christians filling his province.

“They met before dawn and sang a hymn to Christ, as to a god.”

Within eighty years of his death, they were already worshipping him. Long before any council decided anything.

Pliny the Younger, Letters 10.96

Suetonius wrote the lives of the emperors. He noted that around 49 AD, Jews were expelled from Rome for rioting over someone he calls “Chrestus.”

The New Testament records the very same expulsion. Two sources, one Christian, one not, landing on the same event.

Suetonius, Claudius 25.4 · Acts 18:2

A Syrian philosopher named Mara bar Serapion, writing to his son, asked what the Jews gained by “executing their wise king.”

And the Jewish Talmud, no friend of Jesus, still admits it. It says he was executed on the eve of Passover for “sorcery.” Their own way of conceding he did things they could not explain.

Babylonian Talmud, Sanhedrin 43a

Even Lucian, a Greek satirist who mocked Christians as gullible, granted the basic facts.

“They worship a man to this day, the one who introduced their novel rites, and was crucified on that account.”

Celsus, another hostile Greek critic, tried to explain Jesus’ miracles away as magic. He never once said Jesus did not exist.

Lucian, The Death of Peregrinus

Add it up. Without opening a Bible, from writers who wanted the movement gone, we already know this.

Jesus was a real man. A teacher in first-century Judea. With a large following. Credited with wonders his enemies called sorcery. He had a brother named James. He was executed under Pontius Pilate. His followers worshipped him within decades.

That is more independent testimony than we have for most of the ancient world.

Now add the New Testament back. Not as scripture. As evidence.

It is not one book. It is 27 documents, written by many hands, in three languages, within decades of the events.

No historian throws out a source simply for being religious.

Paul’s letters are the earliest we have. Fifteen to thirty years after the cross.

And Paul says he met them in person. Peter. John. And James, the brother of Jesus.

You do not sit down to eat with the brother of a legend.

Galatians 1:19

The four Gospels were written within a single generation. Mark within about 35 years of the events.

There was no gap of centuries for a legend to grow in the dark. The people who knew Jesus were still alive to correct the record.

And they read like testimony, not folklore. Real people are named. Bartimaeus. Simon of Cyrene. Joseph of Arimathea.

So are details no inventor would keep. Peter denying he ever knew him. The disciples scattering in fear. Women as the first witnesses, in a world that would not accept a woman’s word in court.

Inventors tidy those away. Witnesses leave them in.

And the text was preserved better than any book of its age.

Over 5,800 Greek copies survive. Over 25,000 in every language. The earliest fragment sits within about fifty years of the original.

Homer’s Iliad, the next best preserved, has around 1,800 copies. The works of Tacitus survive in about twenty. By the standard used for every other ancient book, this one stands alone.

“But Christianity just copied older myths. Mithras. Horus. Osiris.”

Read the actual sources. Mithras was born from a rock, not a virgin. Horus was never crucified. None of them is a real, dated, eyewitness-claimed man of history.

That theory came from an internet documentary, not a library. Scholars retired it long ago.

So the myth falls apart at every point.

Jesus of Nazareth was a real man who walked the earth. That was never the honest question. The honest question is who he was.

“We did not follow cleverly invented stories when we told you about the coming of our Lord Jesus Christ. We were eyewitnesses of his majesty.”

2 Peter 1:16

The Historical Jesus

Did Jesus really die?

Swipe →

There are two ways people dodge the cross.

Maybe he did not really die. He only fainted, and revived later. Or maybe it was not even him up there, and someone else was killed in his place.

Either way, no death, no resurrection, no Christianity. So the question is not small.

Start with the skeptics.

“One of the most certain facts of history is that Jesus was crucified on orders of the Roman prefect of Judea, Pontius Pilate.”

That is Bart Ehrman, an agnostic. The atheist scholar Gerd Lüdemann goes just as far, calling the death by crucifixion “indisputable.”

Bart Ehrman

Historians rank his death among the most certain facts of the ancient world. More certain than events they accept without a second thought.

Four reasons carry that weight.

First, the sheer number of witnesses.

His death is recorded in all four Gospels, in the earliest creed within a few years, in Paul’s letters, in Acts, and in Hebrews. Then outside the church entirely: Tacitus, Josephus, the Talmud, Lucian, Mara bar Serapion.

Eight to ten independent sources for a single event. Almost nothing in ancient history is attested that well.

Second, the shame of it.

Crucifixion was the ugliest death Rome had. Reserved for slaves and rebels. If you were inventing a Messiah, the last thing you would invent is a crucified one.

The first Christians did not bury that fact. They preached it. You do not invent your own disgrace.

Third, the men who did it.

Rome crucified thousands. These were professional executioners. A soldier who let a condemned man down alive paid for it with his own life.

They did not make mistakes about death.

Jesus died faster than most. Hours, not days.

It was quick enough that Pilate himself was surprised to hear he was already dead. He summoned the centurion to confirm it before releasing the body.

Even the man who signed the order made sure.

Mark 15:44

The standard way to hasten death was to break the victim’s legs, so he could no longer push up to breathe.

When the soldiers reached Jesus, they saw he was already dead. So they left his legs unbroken.

The executioners themselves certified him gone.

John 19:33

To be certain, one of them drove a spear into his side.

“and at once blood and water came out.”

A fisherman, writing decades later for ordinary readers, recorded a medical detail he had no reason, and no way, to invent.

John 19:34

Modern medicine recognises what he described.

In 1986, pathologists in the Journal of the American Medical Association examined the crucifixion. Their finding was blunt.

“Jesus was dead when taken down from the cross.”

The flow of blood and water is exactly what a body already dead would produce. He was gone before the spear ever touched him.

JAMA, 1986

So he died. That much is history. Now the two ways people try to escape it.

The first is the swoon theory. The claim that Jesus never really died. He only passed out on the cross, and woke again in the cool of the tomb.

It was abandoned back in the 1800s. And not by Christians.

The skeptic David Strauss, no friend of the faith, buried this theory himself.

A man half-dead, whipped to the bone, pierced through, and sealed in a tomb for three days with no care, could not roll away a massive stone, overpower armed guards, and walk miles on pierced feet.

He would have looked like a man who needed a hospital. Not the risen Lord of life.

The second escape came six hundred years later.

The Qur’an says it only appeared that he was crucified.

“They did not kill him, nor did they crucify him, but it was made to appear so to them.”

Qur’an 4:157

There is not one ancient source behind this. Not one.

It appears six centuries after the event, in another land, written by someone who was not there.

The people who were there, his mother, the women, his disciples, watched him die up close. The idea that every one of them mistook another man for Jesus is impossible to believe.

Look who stood at that cross.

His mother, at the very foot of it. The women who had followed him from Galilee, watching nearby. His enemies, who wanted him dead and stayed to see it done.

Romans. Jews. Friends. Enemies. All of them agree on one fact. He died.

And he was buried.

Taken down, wrapped in linen, and laid in a known tomb belonging to Joseph of Arimathea, a member of the very council that had condemned him.

A named man. A known grave. There was no confusion about whether he had died.

So weigh it honestly.

Friends recorded his death. Enemies confirmed it. A Roman governor was surprised by how fast it came. Modern doctors verified it. Every escape theory was built on hope, not evidence.

By any standard we use for history, this one is settled.

Jesus of Nazareth was crucified under Pontius Pilate, and he died.

A dead man. Certified by the very soldiers paid to kill him. Sealed in a borrowed grave.

That is not the end of the story. That is where it begins.

It was real. It was finished. It was witnessed.

“When Jesus had received the drink, he said, ‘It is finished.’ With that, he bowed his head and gave up his spirit.”

John 19:30

The Historical Jesus

Was Jesus raised from the dead?

Swipe →

Every other question so far has been leading here.

Christianity does not simply include the resurrection. It stands or falls on it. Paul said so himself.

“If Christ has not been raised, our preaching is useless, and so is your faith.”

1 Corinthians 15:14

Most people assume the resurrection is off limits to history. A matter of faith, not evidence. It is not.

The scholar Gary Habermas spent decades reading thousands of academic works on the subject. He found a short list of facts that nearly every critical scholar accepts. Atheist, agnostic, Jewish, Christian.

Then he asked the only question that matters. What explains all of them at once?

Fact one. Jesus died by crucifixion.

We have already seen it. Friends recorded it, enemies confirmed it, doctors verified it. On this, there is no serious debate.

So the man was truly dead. Start there.

Fact two, and the strongest of all. His disciples were certain they had seen him alive again.

This is not one late report. It runs through the earliest creed, all four Gospels, the sermons in Acts, and the rest of their lives.

Whatever happened, they were sure they had seen him.

You do not have to take a believer’s word for it. Take the skeptics.

“We can say with complete certainty that some of his disciples insisted that he soon appeared to them, convincing them that he had been raised from the dead.”

That is Bart Ehrman, an agnostic. The scholar E.P. Sanders agrees. That the followers had resurrection experiences is, in his words, “a fact.”

Bart Ehrman · E.P. Sanders

Now feel the weight of it.

These were not people repeating a rumour. They claimed to see him with their own eyes. And almost every one of them, by the earliest accounts we have, was killed rather than take it back.

People will die for what they believe is true. Nobody dies for what they know is a lie.

Fact three. Paul.

He was not a follower. He was hunting Christians down, dragging them to prison. Then on the road to Damascus, he said he met the risen Jesus.

He spent the rest of his life beaten, stoned, shipwrecked, and finally executed for it. He had everything to lose and nothing to gain.

Fact four. James, the brother of Jesus.

During Jesus’ life, his own brothers did not believe in him. Then, after the cross, James becomes a leader of the church in Jerusalem, and dies a martyr for it.

The earliest creed says simply, “he appeared to James.” Something turned a skeptical brother into a man who would die for him.

Fact five. The tomb was empty.

This is the most debated of the five, yet still granted by around three quarters of scholars.

And the detail that seals it. Everyone knew where the tomb was. To end the movement overnight, the authorities only had to produce the body. They never did.

Look at how his enemies answered.

The earliest counter-story, recorded in Matthew, was not “the tomb is full.” It was “his disciples came and stole the body.”

Even the opposition conceded the tomb was empty. They only argued about how.

Here is one of the strongest clues of all.

In that world, a woman’s testimony was not accepted in court. If you were inventing a resurrection story, you would never make women your first witnesses. You would put Peter and John there, to make it stand up.

Instead, all four Gospels say women found the tomb, and Mary Magdalene saw him first. The only reason to write it that way is that it is what happened.

And none of this grew slowly over centuries.

Paul hands on a creed he says he “received” and “passed on.”

“that Christ died for our sins, that he was buried, that he was raised on the third day, and that he appeared to Peter, and then to the Twelve.”

Scholars date it to within a few years of the cross. Some, within months. It names living witnesses, and all but says: go and ask them.

1 Corinthians 15:3-5

So here are the five, agreed by friend and skeptic alike.

He died. His disciples were certain they saw him. Paul was certain. James was certain. The tomb was empty.

Now the honest test. Can any ordinary explanation carry all five? Watch them fall, one by one.

Maybe they hallucinated.

But hallucinations are private. They do not happen to groups. The creed lists Peter, then the Twelve, then five hundred at once, then James, then all the apostles.

Group hallucinations are unknown to psychology. And a hallucination cannot empty a tomb.

Maybe the disciples stole the body.

Then they died, one after another, under torture, for something they knew was a fraud.

People do not do that. Their willingness to die is proof they truly believed it.

Maybe the women went to the wrong tomb.

Then the authorities walk to the right one and produce the body. The rumour dies that afternoon.

They had every reason to. They never did.

Maybe it was a legend that grew with time.

But the creed is within a few years of the events. There was no time for legend to form.

The resurrection was the message from the very first day, not a later addition.

So line them up.

He died. His disciples were sure they saw him. An enemy, Paul, was sure. A skeptic, James, was sure. The tomb was empty.

Every natural theory explains one or two of these, and breaks on the rest. Only one explanation accounts for all five at once. He rose.

This is not wishful thinking. It is where the evidence leads even careful historians.

“The historian, of whatever persuasion, has no option but to affirm both the empty tomb and the meetings with Jesus as historical events.”

N.T. Wright spent a thousand pages weighing it. His conclusion: the best explanation is that Jesus was bodily alive again.

N.T. Wright

You can still say no.

But not on the evidence. You would be saying no because your worldview rules out miracles before you begin. That is a philosophical choice, not a historical one.

The history is serious, and it points one way.

And if it happened, everything changes.

A man who beat the grave is not one teacher among many. Everything he said about himself is true.

This is not a story to admire from a distance. It is a fact to reckon with.

The tomb was empty. The witnesses were sure. The grave lost.

“He is not here. He has risen, just as he said.”

Luke 24:6

Messianic Prophecy

Was Jesus foretold?

Swipe →

Christians say the Old Testament predicted Jesus. Skeptics have two comebacks.

Either the prophecies were written after the fact. Or Jesus simply staged his life to match them.

Both have answers. And the evidence is older than you think.

It only matters if the prophecies came first. So start there.

In 1947, a shepherd found the Dead Sea Scrolls in a cave near the Dead Sea. Among them was a complete scroll of Isaiah, dated to about 125 BC.

Over a hundred years before Jesus was born. Every messianic line in it was already ancient when he arrived.

And these texts were not kept by Christians.

They were copied and guarded for centuries by Jewish scribes, who did not accept Jesus as Messiah.

Nobody slipped these predictions in later. They were there all along, held by the very people with every reason to remove them.

So the prophecies are old, and untouched. Now hear what they say.

They named the town. Seven hundred years before, Micah pointed to one small village.

“But you, Bethlehem, though you are small among the clans of Judah, out of you will come one who will be ruler over Israel.”

Micah 5:2

Isaiah gave the sign of his birth.

“The virgin will conceive and give birth to a son, and will call him Immanuel.”

Immanuel means God with us. Written seven centuries early.

Isaiah 7:14

Zechariah described how the king would enter his city. Not on a war horse. Humble, on a donkey.

“See, your king comes to you, righteous and victorious, lowly and riding on a donkey.”

Days before he died, Jesus rode into Jerusalem exactly so.

Zechariah 9:9

Zechariah even named the price of the betrayal.

Thirty pieces of silver, thrown to the potter in the house of the Lord.

Jesus was betrayed for thirty pieces of silver. The coins were thrown down in the temple, and used to buy a potter’s field.

Zechariah 11:12-13

But the deepest prophecy is Isaiah 53. Written 700 years before the cross.

For years, skeptics claimed Christians had edited it in later, to make Jesus fit.

Then the 125 BC scroll was found, with Isaiah 53 already in it, untouched. That argument was over.

Read what it says.

“He was despised and rejected by men, a man of suffering. He was pierced for our transgressions, he was crushed for our iniquities.”

A suffering, rejected, pierced servant. Centuries before Rome nailed a single man to a cross.

Isaiah 53:3, 5

It kept going.

“He was oppressed and afflicted, yet he did not open his mouth. He was assigned a grave with the wicked, and with the rich in his death.”

Jesus stood silent before his accusers. He was crucified between criminals, and buried in a rich man’s tomb.

Isaiah 53:7, 9

Now the most startling one. Psalm 22, written by David around 1000 BC.

It describes an execution in detail, centuries before crucifixion was even invented.

“They pierce my hands and my feet. They divide my clothes among them and cast lots for my garment. All who see me mock me.”

Pierced hands and feet. Soldiers gambling for his clothing. A watching crowd that mocked. Every line happened at the cross.

Psalm 22:7, 16, 18

Skeptics once argued the word was “pierced” only in Christian copies. A Dead Sea Psalms scroll settled it. The reading “pierced my hands and my feet” was there before Christ.

And another line: “not one of his bones will be broken.”

The soldiers broke the legs of the two men beside Jesus, to hasten death. When they reached him, he was already dead, so they left his bones unbroken.

Psalm 34:20

Then comes the boldest prophecy of them all. Daniel did not just say where, and how.

He said when.

Daniel wrote that from the decree to rebuild Jerusalem, there would be 483 years until the Messiah, and then he would be “cut off.” Killed.

The decree went out around 444 BC. Counting those years forward lands in the early first century, around AD 33. The very time Jesus was crucified.

Scholars debate the exact decree and the calendar used. But every reasonable method lands in the same window, within Jesus’ lifetime.

Daniel 9:25-26

And Daniel added a line that closes the door.

The Messiah would come and be cut off before the city and the temple were destroyed.

The temple fell in AD 70. So the Messiah had to have already come. Any claimant after that is simply too late. The window was open for one generation, and Jesus stepped into it.

One mathematician, Peter Stoner, tried to put a number on all this.

Take just eight of these prophecies. He estimated the odds of one man fulfilling all eight by chance at one in ten to the seventeenth power.

These are estimates, not exact science. But even generously, the improbability is hard to wave away.

To feel that number, Stoner painted a picture.

Cover the whole state of Texas two feet deep in silver dollars. Mark one single coin, and bury it somewhere in the pile. Blindfold a man, and send him to pick the right one on his first try.

That is the chance. And Jesus fulfilled far more than eight.

So return to the skeptic.

Written after the fact? The scrolls are older than Jesus, kept by his opponents.

Staged by Jesus? You cannot arrange your own birthplace, your ancestral line, a betrayal price set centuries before, a timeline fixed in Daniel, or the details of an execution not yet invented. Some things are beyond staging.

This is what design looks like.

One story, told across a thousand years, by dozens of writers who never met, all pointing to one man.

Then the man arrived. On time. In the right town. And died exactly as it was written.

You can still call it coincidence.

But at some point, coincidence becomes the harder thing to believe.

Written for centuries. Fulfilled in one life.

“Everything must be fulfilled that is written about me in the Law of Moses, the Prophets and the Psalms.”

Luke 24:44

The Gospels

Who wrote the Gospels?

Swipe →

Skeptics like to say the Gospels are anonymous, written centuries later by unknown hands. So you cannot trust them.

Both halves are false. Start with the timing.

Mark was written within about 35 years of the cross. Matthew within 50. Luke within 55.

All of them within the lifetime of people who were there. Some of them by eyewitnesses themselves.

To feel how close that is, compare it to anyone else.

The main biographies of Alexander the Great were written about 400 years after he died. Nobody doubts Alexander existed.

The Gospels stand eight to ten times closer to their subject than that.

“But they are anonymous. The authors never sign them.”

True. So did most ancient biographies. What matters is who the ancient world said wrote them. And on that, there is no argument at all.

Papias around AD 125. Irenaeus around 180. The Muratorian list. Origen, Tertullian, Clement.

Every early source, in every region, names the same authors. There is no rival tradition. Nobody in the ancient world ever proposed anyone else.

And here is the giveaway.

If the church wanted to invent authority, it would have chosen famous names. Peter. James. Andrew.

Instead it named a tax collector, an assistant of Peter, and a Gentile doctor. Forgers pick big names. Truth-tellers report who actually wrote it.

Take Mark. The oldest testimony comes from Papias, who knew disciples of the apostle John.

“Mark, the interpreter of Peter, wrote down accurately whatever he remembered of the things said and done by the Lord. He took special care not to omit anything, and not to state anything falsely.”

Papias, in Eusebius 3.39.15

And Mark reads exactly like that. Short, fast, vivid, full of an eyewitness’s small details.

He pauses to explain Jewish customs his Roman readers would not know. He is writing down Peter’s memory. Mark is Peter’s voice, put on paper.

Then Matthew. A tax collector, called by Jesus from his booth.

And a tax collector was the one man in that group trained to write, keep records, and take notes. If anyone was writing things down as they happened, it was him.

His Gospel proves the fit. Written for Jews. Packed with more Old Testament quotations than any other, over sixty of them. Tracing Jesus back to Abraham, the father of the Jewish people.

A Jewish hand, writing for a Jewish audience.

Then Luke. A doctor, a Gentile, a travelling companion of Paul. And unlike the others, he opens like a working historian.

“Having had perfect understanding of all things from the very first, I write to you, that you might know the certainty of the things you have been taught.”

Luke 1:3-4

Luke also wrote Acts, where at times he says “we,” because he was there.

As Paul’s companion he could sit with Peter, with James, with Mary the mother of Jesus. He interviewed the witnesses. He checked his facts.

So look at what you actually have.

One Gospel from Peter’s own memory. One from an apostle trained to take notes. One from a historian who interviewed the survivors.

All three within a generation. All three named by everyone who came after.

These are not anonymous legends written centuries later.

They are the church’s first witnesses, writing down what they saw and heard, while the memory was still warm.

Not a rumour handed down. Testimony, written by the people who were there.

“That which was from the beginning, which we have heard, which we have seen with our eyes, which our hands have touched, this we proclaim to you.”

1 John 1:1

The Gospels

Do the Gospels contradict?

Swipe →

This is the most common attack of all. The Gospels disagree with each other. Different numbers of angels. Different women at the tomb. Two different family trees.

It sounds devastating. It is actually backwards.

Ask any detective.

If four witnesses to a crash tell the exact same story, in the exact same words, he suspects they rehearsed it. Real witnesses notice different things. One sees the colour of the car. One sees the driver. One reads the plate.

Agreement in the main, difference in the details. That is what truth sounds like.

A Harvard law professor named Simon Greenleaf wrote the standard textbook on legal evidence in his century.

He put the Gospels on trial by the rules of a courtroom. His verdict: they would stand up in any court as reliable testimony, precisely because they vary.

Their differences prove they did not copy.

So take the hard cases directly. First, the angels at the tomb.

Matthew says one angel. Luke says two. A contradiction? No. If there were two, there was also one. Matthew simply focused on the one who spoke.

Mentioning one does not deny the other. Matthew’s account fits inside Luke’s.

Next, the women.

One Gospel names Mary Magdalene alone. Another names several women. Also no contradiction. They came over the course of a morning, in groups, at different times, and saw different things.

Every account agrees on the core. The tomb was empty. Angels announced it. Women were first.

Now the hardest-looking one. The two genealogies.

Matthew and Luke both trace Jesus’ family line. And after King David, the two lists diverge completely.

The oldest explanation still holds. Matthew gives the legal, royal line through Joseph. Luke gives the biological line through Mary.

It fits everything at once. It explains why Matthew starts at Abraham, the father of the Jews, while Luke goes back to Adam, the father of everyone.

Two purposes. Two lines. No contradiction.

Then the temple cleansing. John puts it early in Jesus’ ministry. The other three put it late.

Two reasonable answers. Either Jesus did it twice, at the start and the end, which fits his zeal. Or a writer arranged his material by theme rather than strict order.

That last point matters more than people realise.

Ancient readers did not expect a minute-by-minute timeline. Writers grouped events by subject and meaning. We read that as an error only because we expect a precision they never promised.

The most technical one is the Passover timing. The first three seem to call the Last Supper the Passover meal. John seems to place the cross as the lambs were being killed, a day earlier.

Careful scholars give real answers. Different Jewish groups used slightly different calendars. Or “the Passover” named the whole multi-day feast.

The difficulty is honest. So are the solutions. And the core fact stands untouched. Jesus was crucified at Passover.

Now notice what these differences never touch.

That Jesus lived. That he taught. That he was crucified under Pilate. That his tomb was found empty.

On every claim that actually matters, the Gospels speak with one voice.

And picture the alternative.

If these books were forged together in one room, the details would line up perfectly. Same angels. Same women. Same order.

They do not. That roughness is the sound of four men remembering the same real events from four different seats.

So the differences you were told break the Gospels are the very thing that proves them.

Smooth, matching stories are the invented ones. Real memory is always a little rough.

Many voices. One story. That is what witnesses sound like.

“Every matter must be established by the testimony of two or three witnesses.”

2 Corinthians 13:1

The Gospels

Are the Gospels eyewitness accounts?

Swipe →

Skeptics will grant the Gospels are old, and still say they are just stories the church told. Legend, dressed as history.

But look closely, and you find fingerprints no forger could plant. Marks left only by people who were actually there.

Start with what scholars call undesigned coincidences.

One Gospel drops a small, pointless detail. Another Gospel, with no connection and no comment, accidentally explains it. Puzzle pieces cut from different boxes that somehow fit.

At the feeding of the five thousand, Mark says the crowd sat on the “green grass.” Why green? He never says.

John, telling the same story, never mentions grass. But he notes it was near Passover. Passover is spring, just after the rains, the one short window the Galilee hills turn green.

Neither writer explains the other. Together they lock.

Mark 6:39 · John 6:4

At that same feeding, Jesus asks Philip where to buy bread. Why Philip, out of twelve? John never says.

But another line notes Philip was from Bethsaida. And Luke says the feeding happened near Bethsaida. Jesus asked the local.

You have to read three separate passages to even see it.

John 6:5 · John 1:44 · Luke 9:10

Matthew says Jesus healed Peter’s mother-in-law, which quietly means Peter had a wife, though Matthew never mentions her.

Twenty years later, in a letter about something else entirely, Paul mentions the apostles travelling with their wives, Peter among them.

Two writers. Two contexts. One fact. Neither one trying to prove it.

Matthew 8:14 · 1 Corinthians 9:5

Matthew says Herod heard detailed news of Jesus. How would a Roman-appointed ruler know so much about a wandering Galilean rabbi? Matthew never says.

But Luke, listing Jesus’ supporters, names Joanna, wife of the manager of Herod’s household. Herod’s own court had an insider.

The detail sits there, unexplained, until Luke’s line completes it.

Matthew 14:1 · Luke 8:3

These run all through the Gospels. One scholar’s book documents dozens of them.

You cannot plan this. A forger either explains a detail or leaves it out. Only real witnesses drop random true details that quietly fit together years later.

Now a discovery from 2006. The scholar Richard Bauckham studied how common each Jewish name was in first-century Palestine, drawing on tombstones, Josephus, and rabbinic records.

The most common names were Simon, Joseph, Judah, John. Then he checked the Gospels.

The names in the Gospels match that exact distribution, precisely. But Jews living elsewhere, in Egypt for instance, used a different set of names.

A forger writing later, in another country, would have gotten them wrong. The Gospels get them right.

Bauckham saw something more.

When the Gospels name a minor figure, Bartimaeus, Simon of Cyrene, Joanna, Cleopas, it is often because that person was the source.

The name works like a footnote. It says: if you doubt this, go and ask them.

Jesus spoke Aramaic. The Gospels were written in Greek, for the wider world. Yet Aramaic keeps surfacing, his own words, left untranslated, then explained for the reader.

“Talitha cumi.” “Ephphatha.” “Eloi, Eloi, lama sabachthani.”

Mark 5:41 · 7:34 · 15:34

A later Greek forger would not sprinkle in random Aramaic phrases.

But if you were remembering what Jesus actually said, in the voice he said it, his own words would break through in his own language.

That is memory, not invention.

And every time the Gospels make a claim you can check, the ground keeps backing them up.

For centuries skeptics said Pontius Pilate might be a fiction.

Then in 1961, in Caesarea, archaeologists found a stone carved with his name and his exact title. Pontius Pilate, Prefect of Judea.

Precisely as the Gospels had him.

In 1990 they found the bone box of Caiaphas, the high priest who tried Jesus.

The Pool of Bethesda, long dismissed as legend, was dug up with its five covered walkways, exactly as John described. Nazareth, said not to exist, turned up as a first-century village.

A Scottish scholar, Sir William Ramsay, set out to prove Luke a careless inventor. He spent decades digging in the very places Luke named. He came back and wrote this.

“Luke is a historian of the first rank. This author should be placed along with the very greatest of historians.”

Sir William Ramsay

What broke Ramsay was the small things.

Luke got every regional official’s title right, in every province, at the correct year, titles that shifted from place to place and over time.

A forger fails that test. An eyewitness with careful notes passes it.

So add it up.

Details that interlock by accident. Names only a local would know. Aramaic breaking through the Greek. Stones and pools and titles confirming the story.

This is not how legends read. It is how memory reads.

They wrote what they saw. And the ground still agrees with them.

“The man who saw it has given testimony, and his testimony is true. He knows that he tells the truth.”

John 19:35
